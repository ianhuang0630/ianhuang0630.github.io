Travicus: Travis, but after puberty.

Travicus is a work in progress. Travis was nice, but I found a few aspects wanting during day-to-day use:

1) The 3D printed enclosure, though uniquely in-house, breaks frequently from mechanical vibrations.

2) Speaking of vibrations, the quality of Stanford's roads has super high variance. I've had to pay far more attention than I'd like to the quality of roads I skate on, as excessively uneven and worn roads causes high discomfort

3) The single Turnigy Outrunner motor that I've been using strains at startup, requiring me to give Travis a good push at the beginning. If we're gonna use an electric skateboard, lets be lazy all the way; let's get rid of the need for that push.

4) The board's compactness is great when it comes to mounting at the back of my backpack, but it comes with a higher risk at high speeds. The increased instability is definitely a downside.

Travicus is going to come with some new features that gets rid of these issues. For one, this time around its parts will be either machined out of sheetmetal or cut out of a strong material using a laser cutter. It will also be a four-wheel drive instead of a one-wheel drive (*), which should give it more than enough power haul my bodyweight. To combat the terrible asphalt conditions at Stanford, I'll be equipping it with cloud suspension wheels.

Here's the interesting part though: Travicus will be equipped with a mechanism that will allow it to range in length from a compact 25 inches to a stabler 35-40 inches.

If you're interested in this idea and wanna help me make it a reality, shoot me an email!

* which may lend itself to software that steers the skateboard automatically ...? I've been dreaming for a long time about a skateboard that can follow you as you as you're walking, and for that to happen on a one-wheel drive, a steering mechanism would have to be designed. With a four-wheel drive, perhaps differential speeds can get the job done.

Travis: Building an electric skateboard from scratch

To quote Feynman, "I cannot create, I do not understand". Against the warning of everyone who were not blinded by my ignorance optimism, I set out to create my own electric skateboard using circuits I design and solder myself, as well as 3d printed parts that I CADded (CADed?) on Fusion360. Starting out with very little background in hardware and electronics, I found it to be a very tough ride (pun intended). But countless broken 3D-printed parts and fried electrical circuits later, the project was finished.

I've been using it to get around campus at Stanford. Here's a couple pictures of it in action. As you can see, its compactness allows it to be mounted at the back of a backpack to free up the hands, and the mace-like appearance of the controller keeps the bad guys on their toes. I've been thrown off of it a couple times (embarrassingly, sometimes infront of complete strangers) at the beginning due to poorly written firmware, but that quickly got smoothed over.

I have never topped out on speed (small boards tend to be a lot less stable), and have also never exhausted its battery on a single trip. I've used all the cheapest components for this prototype, and I think all parts added together would not cost more than $200. If you're interested in doing something similar, shoot me an email and I'd be happy to share code, circuit designs, CAD files and lessons I learned the hard way.

* including the backlit display of my beloved macbook.. God bless his soul. Ask me if you're curious what happened.

Motorized Camera

With the rise of Zoom as the main way of communication during the coronavirus pandemic, a webcam is a must. But searching through the products available on the market, I was unsatsified. I wanted a 2-DOF motorized camera that I can control to look anywhere I want. This saves me the trouble of having to get up from my chair to turn the camera up to the ceiling when I'm done with calls (for hackers/NSA). I know that practically, it was going to be quite useless, but this itch of an idea existed to be scratched.

The whole project took half a day from design to 3d printing and assembly.
